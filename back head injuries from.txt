how does a hoverboard work then verses how it should work?

How It Actually Works vs. How It Should Work
Sensing — What It Does
The board reads raw pitch angle from two cheap, independent IMUs (one per side). No filtering. Every micro-twitch your leg makes, every pebble it hits, gets treated as a real intentional input. The footpad detection is two plastic tabs blocking an infrared beam — binary on/off, no nuance.

Sensing — How It Should Work
A Kalman filter or moving average should sit between the raw sensor and the control loop, stripping out high-frequency noise (leg shaking, surface vibration) and only passing through deliberate weight shifts. The footpad should require sustained contact for ~150-200ms before engaging anything.

Balancing — What It Does
A bare PID loop chases zero degrees. It reacts to everything instantly and identically, whether you're intentionally leaning or just sneezing. The two halves operate as completely independent vehicles connected by a mechanical pivot, so a bump under one wheel creates a twist the other half doesn't know happened.

Balancing — How It Should Work
The two halves should be treated as one unified system. Sensor data from both sides should be fused together before any motor commands go out. The PID targets should be filtered and smoothed so the board distinguishes between deliberate lean and noise.

Braking — What It Does
When an error threshold is crossed or power drops, the stock firmware runs essentially:

if (error) { shutdown(); }
Power to the MOSFETs cuts instantly. The motors go limp or lock depending on design. The board stops being a vehicle and becomes a stationary obstacle under a moving human body.

Braking — How It Should Work
Three rules, as the conversation correctly identified:

Keep the platform stable — never drop the PID loop mid-ride
No hard braking — slew-rate limit deceleration to something like 1-2 mph per second
No backward roll — electronically gate reverse motor current to zero during any safety event
All three are achievable in software on the existing hardware.

Steering — What It Does
The mechanical pivot lets each foot twist independently. This creates differential steering through pure mechanics — no algorithm needed. It's cheap and works for spinning in place but creates a genuine danger point when one side gets an unexpected input (bump, crack, hesitation) while moving forward, because the board interprets it as a turn command.

Steering — How It Should Work
A solid deck with fused roll-axis sensing. Leaning right accelerates the right wheel proportionally, creating a smooth forward curve like a bicycle. No pivot means no unexpected twist from uneven surfaces.

The Core Problem in One Sentence
The board is built around the hardware being smart enough to handle the balancing, when in reality the hardware is fine but the software is doing the absolute minimum to save fractions of a cent per unit — and people are hitting the back of their skulls on concrete because of it.
